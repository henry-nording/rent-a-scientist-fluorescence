Hydrodynamic focusing

Too many particles, or a poorly focused stream, increases coincidences: two particles reach the laser gate too close together.

sheath fluid flushing 15 percent squeeze low abort rate

Switch on the laser to interrogate the stream. At the measurement point the laser crosses the focused cells: forward scatter (FSC) is collected almost straight ahead of the laser, just past a small beam stop that blocks the direct beam, while side scatter (SSC) is collected at 90 degrees. The little FSC and SSC scopes show the live signal pulse each cell produces as it passes; coincident cells stack into larger pulses. Fluorescence is commonly collected through the same side optics as SSC and then split among additional detectors.

From fluorescence to cell markers

Many cell types look very similar. To tell them apart, flow cytometry searches for characteristic molecules called markers. Fluorescent dyes, called fluorophores, turn these otherwise invisible markers into measurable light signals.

What does this look like on a cell?

Imagine the cell membrane as the cell's protective outer covering. Tiny molecular “antennae” project from it. These are cell-surface markers; many of them are receptors that receive signals from the cell's surroundings.

1. The target marker: The receptor shown here crosses the membrane and extends outside the cell. One example is CD4, a marker found on certain immune cells.

2. The antibody's gripping arms: An antibody is shaped roughly like a large Y. The tips of its two arms are binding sites. Each fits only one particular small region of its target marker, much like a key fitting a lock. When the correct antibody finds its marker, one arm attaches to it.

3. The fluorescent dye: A fluorophore is chemically coupled to the antibody while its binding sites remain free. In this simplified drawing it is attached to the antibody stem. In real laboratory reagents, it may also be coupled at other suitable positions.

4. The measurable signal: When the stained cell passes through the cytometer's laser, the fluorophore absorbs energy and emits light. A detector records this light. The signal tells the computer: “This cell carries the marker we searched for.”

Markers inside a cell can also be measured. For this, the cell membrane must first be made permeable so the antibodies can enter.

Five example immune markers

In this simplified whole-blood panel, CD3 and CD19 identify most T and B cells. CD56 is enriched on NK cells but also appears on subsets of T cells and monocytes. CD14 varies across monocyte subsets and can be dim on granulocytes, while CD66b marks granulocytes. The plots deliberately include these overlaps because populations are identified using combinations of markers rather than one marker alone.
